- In Luxembourg, housing communities are becoming increasingly popular.
- There is a distinction between two types:
- Either people share a dwelling or a house with other people and have only one room for themselves,
- or they live in a residence together with others and have access to various communal spaces.
- Especially young people often share a dwelling,
- to live a bit more affordably and also to have social contact.
- But there are also projects for housing communities for seniors that are increasingly found.
- Here the aim is that elderly people can live autonomously for as long as possible
- and still not be isolated.
- They have their own dwelling,
- but can also benefit from communal spaces.
- Everyday life is made easier for them, so that they can receive help locally
- and possibly benefit from care services.
- Such projects also often have an ecological aspect,
- because a lot of value is placed on proximity
- and also ensure that public transport is guaranteed.
- In another housing form, people share ratios.
- The goal here is primarily to prevent isolation of older people.
- This form of living promotes solidarity between generations.
- This can also be linked with social housing,
- when, for example, in a residence there are social apartments for older people with low incomes
- and small student apartments on the other side.
- Generations live under one roof
- and can help each other socially.
- The elderly can, for example, cook for the younger ones from time to time
- and the youngsters can help with computer problems or go shopping.
- Another form of solidarity living is found in projects
- where people who have applied for asylum in Luxembourg are taken in by families
- instead of having to live in a foyer.
- They can often live with families for free
- and are thus able to help in the house and at home.
- This promotes their integration
- and contributes to a better mutual understanding.
